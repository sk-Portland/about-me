Let's Explore Neurogenesis in Adults

What is neurogenesis?

Neurogenesis is the division of new brain cells. In this case, we are specifically refering to the brain.

Animal studies

Some of the more interesting findings is that adult animals often have new brain cells (and an increase in size) of parts of the brain during seasons associated with a behavior. For example, birds that sing to attract mates have new brain cells in the hippocampus during this time period.

Studies in primates and rats suggest that stress is associated with a decrease in neurogenesis, or fewer new brain cells are born during high stress.

But what about humans?

Studies have found that even elderly humans (in their 70s and 80s) have new new brain cells in a part of the brain devoted to the formation of new memory, the hippocampus.

Let's look closer at the science

Parts of the brain that have been found to have neurogenesis in adulthood:

  • Olfactory bulb
  • Hippocampus
  • Cells lining the cerebral ventricles

      • Methodology to demonstrate neurogenesis (in addition to compliance with good clinical practice, such as obtaining signed consent and permissions with human subjects):

      1. Injecting people with a thymdine analog (bromodeoxyuradine or BrdU to label dividing cells.
      2. Upon death of the person, taking the brain, preserving it, and using antibidies tagged to a marker to label cells with BrdU.
      3. Use another marker to label neurons
      4. Using a miscroscope to image sections of the brain to find whether there are cells with both BrdU and neuronal markers.
